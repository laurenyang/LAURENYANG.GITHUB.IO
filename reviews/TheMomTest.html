Book Review

The Mom Test

By Rob Fitzpatrick



Overview
  1. The Mom Test is a discussion framework which encourages indexing on specific customer past experiences, deflecting compliments, and gunning for commitment in conversations while intentionally withholding the idea at hand.
  2. For effective convos, prep beforehand: know what the top 3 things you are trying to learn are, ask questions which put your idea at risk, and know what commitment you are seeking.

Quotes
  • Every time you talk to someone, you should be asking at least one question which has the potential to destroy your currently imagined business.
  • There’s more reliable information in a “meh” than a “Wow!” You can’t build a business on a lukewarm response.
  • If you don’t know what happens next after a product or sales meeting, the meeting was pointless
  • The more they’re giving up, the more seriously you can take what they’re saying
  • If you aren’t finding consistent problems/goals, customer segment isn’t specific enough
  • If you don’t know what you’re trying to learn, you shouldn’t bother having the conversation

Book Notes

  • Chapter 1: The Mom Test

    • The Mom Test, in a nutshell
      • Talk about their life instead of your idea
      • Ask about specifics in the past instead of generics or opinions about the future
      • Talk less, listen more

  • Chapter 2: Avoid Bad Data

    • Avoid complements: they do not bring value to idea validation
    • ⚠️ Signs of a complement
      • During the meeting: “Thanks, I’m glad you like it”
      • After the meeting: “That meeting went really well, we’re getting a lot of positive feedback, everybody I talked to liked the idea”
    • Mitigating a complement: try to get specific, get real data
      • “Why did you like the idea, how much money would it save, how would it fit into your life, how much money would it save, what else have you tried”
      • Deflect, ignore compliment, get back to business
    • Avoid fluff: you need specific, concrete past experiences to anchor to (that’s real data)
      • Generic claims (”I usually/always/never”)
      • Future tense problems (”I would/will”)
      • Hypothetical analysis (”I might/could”)
      • With fluff, people describe themselves as who they want to be, not who they actually are. You need details to bring out the edge cases
    • Feature Requests: need to understand motivation/root cause, but not necessary obey them
    • Questions to dig into feature requests:
      • Why do you want that?
      • What would that let you do?
      • How are you coping with it?
      • Do you think we should push back launch to add that feature, or we can add it later?
      • How would that fit into your day?
    • Questions to dig into emotional insights
      • Tell me more about that/go on
      • That seems to really bug you, I bet there’s a story there
      • What makes it so awful?
      • Why haven’t you been able to fix this already?
      • You seem pretty excited about that, it’s a big deal?
      • Why so happy?

  • Chapter 3: Asking Important Questions

    • “Does this problem matter” questions: start broad, and then zoom in
      • How serious do you take xyz
      • Do you make money from it? Have you tried making money from it?
      • How much time do you spend on it each week?
      • Do you have major aspirations for xyz
      • Which tools/services do you currently use for xyz
      • What are 3 things you are currently doing to improve xyz
    • Risk:
      • Product Risk: Can I build/grow it?
      • Customer/Market Risk: Do they want it? Will they pay me? Are there lots of them?
      • If product risk > market risk, harder to prove business through conversations alone
    • List of 3 Questions: pre-plan the most important things you want to learn given the person you’re talking to

  • Chapter 4: Keep it Casual

    Self explanatory

  • Chapter 5: Commitment and Advancement

    • Symptoms of low commitment
      • Pipeline of zombie leads
      • Product meetings which end in compliments/no clear next steps
      • Meetings which “went well”
      • They haven’t given up anything of value
    • Why you are getting wishy washy signals
      • You’re asking fo their opinion about your idea (ie fishing for compliments)
      • You’re not asking for clear commitment or next steps
    • Examples of time commitments:
      • Clear next meeting with known goals
      • Sitting down to give feedback on wireframes
      • Using trial of product for non trivial amount of time
    • Examples of risk commitments:
      • Intro to peers/teams
      • Intro to decision maker (boss, spouse, lawyer)
      • Giving a public testimonial or case study
    • Financial commitments
      • Letter of intent, preorder, deposit
    • Customers who keep being friendly but aren’t ever going to buy are a particularly dangerous source of mixed signals
    • Strong emotion
      • If someone is not too emotional, they are unlikely to be “crazy first customer”
      • When you see deep emotion, do your utmost to keep that person close. They will go through hard times with you and likely give you your first sell

  • Chapter 6: Finding Conversations

    • Cold emails are a tool to tap into warm intros. Try to phase to warm intros ASAP
    • Cold email template (Vision/Framing/Weakness/Pedestal/Ask):
      1. You’re an entrepreneur trying to solve horrible problem x, usher in wonderful vision y, or fix stagnant industry z. Don’t mention idea.
      2. Frame expectations by mentioning what stage you’re at, and if it’s true, that you don’t have anything to sell.
      3. Show weakness, give them chance to help by mentioning specific problem you’re looking for answers on. Signal you are not time waster
      4. Demonstrate you value their opinion by showing how they in particular can help
      5. Explicitly ask for help
    • It’s hard but important for you to be the driver of the convo - ensure we are on agenda, need to be assertive

  • Chapter 7: Choosing Your Customer

    • Determine which customer segment to first target by: 1) whether it is profitable/big 2) easy to reach

  • Chapter 8: Running the Process

    • Avoid a learning bottleneck:
      1. Prepping: have list of big 3 questions, face scary questions, do research prior to meeting, prep with whole team. Need to answer: what do we want to learn from these guys
      2. Reviewing: review notes and update team. Share quotes, any problems you ran into. Quick questions worked well, what could you do different next time. Dissemenate info as fast as possible with team
      3. Taking good notes
    • Symptoms of learning bottlenecks
      • “You handle the product, I’ll handle the customer”
      • “Because the customers told me so”
      • “I don’t have time to talk to people - I need to keep coding”
    • Usually sending 2 people is sufficient, try not to send more than 2
    • Signs of just going through the motions:
      • You are talking more than they are
      • Compliments
      • No next steps, you told them the idea
      • No notes/no reviewing notes
      • Unexpected answer did not change idea
      • No scary questions
      • Unclear what end goal is of conversation/what are you trying to learn
    • Before conversation checklist
      • Choose a focused, findable segment
      • Decide big 3 learning goals with team
      • Decide on ideal next steps and commitment
      • Figure out who to talk to
      • Create hypothesis about what person cares about
      • Do desk research prior to meeting to try to answer questions
    • During conversation checklist
      • Frame conversation
      • Keep it casual
      • Ask good questions
      • Deflect compliments, anchor fluff, dig beneath signals
      • Take good notes
      • Press for commitment and next steps
    • After conversation checklist
      • Review quotes, notes with team
      • Transfer notes to permanent storage
      • Update belief/plans
      • Decide next big 3 questions
