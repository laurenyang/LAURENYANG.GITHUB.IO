Book Review

Zero to One

By Blake Masters and Peter Thiel



Overview

Thiel shares his philosophy on starting new companies in Zero to One. The book is a playful balance of frameworks, personal anecdotes, and real world examples. Reads easy, like a conversation.

Would recommend for anyone looking for an “Intro to Entrepreneurship”, but did not find anything extremely contrarian ironically enough. Perhaps Thiel’s ideas have become so popularized since the book’s publishing that they now define the norm? In general I’m wary of Malcolm Gladwell-y books which draw sweeping conclusions from 1-2 examples. This book errs on that, but can see this being a useful reference as one starts his/her own thing.

  1. Be a monopoly, even if it means starting in a small, niche market. Monopolies are net benefits to society and business.
  2. Successful founders lead a cult-like company culture and motivate team members toward a shared vision.

Summary
Lessons from the Dot Com Boom
  • Events leading up to the Dot Com bubble (18 months from Sept ‘98 to March ‘00): 1) Netscape IPO Aug ‘95 despite being unprofitable 2) East Asian financial crisis hit July ‘97 3) Ruble crisis in August ‘98 where Russia defaulted on debt 4) Euro launched January ‘99
  • Guiding Principles
    1. Make incremental advances: grand visions inflated the bubble. You should make small, tangible steps forward
    2. Stay lean and flexible: all companies must stay lean/unplanned, allowing you to iterate
    3. Improve on the competition: The only way to know you are a real business is to start with an already existing customer, so you should build your company by improving on recognizable products
    4. Focus on product, not sales: tech is primarily about product dev, not distribution
  • Yet the opposite of these rules is probably also correct

Monopoly vs Competition
  • Creating value is not enough, you need to capture some of the value you create
  • Perfect Competition: in the long run, no firm makes any economic profit. Every firm is undifferentiated, must sell at market price, if there is more money supply, new firms will enter the market and increase supply, drive prices down, therefore eliminating profits
  • Fatal temptation: describe your market as extremely narrow so you dominate by definition
  • Monopoly: a company that is so good at what it does, no other company can offer close substitute
  • Monopolists lie to protect themselves: ie Google claiming to be in many different markets to hide their monopoly in search
  • Creative monopolists give customers more choice by adding entirely new categories of abundance

Characteristics of a Monopoly
  • Proprietary tech: must be x10 better than closest substitute. Easiest way to be x10 better is to invent something new
  • Network effects: product is valuable to first few users even if network is small
  • Economies of scale: gets stronger as it gets bigger - fixed cost of creating a product can be spread over greater quantities of scale
  • Branding: a company has a monopoly over it’s brand by definition, so creating a strong brand is powerful way to claim a monopoly

Building a Monopoly
  • Start small and monopolize - err on the side of starting too small, since it’s easier to dominate a smaller market
  • Scale up - after you dominate a niche market. Sequencing markets is important
  • Don’t disrupt - if your company can be summed by opposition to already existing firms, it’s not completely new and likely not a monopoly. Avoid competition as much as possible

We live in an indefinitely optimistic world:

  • Indefinite finance, indefinite politics, biotech startups are indefinitely optimistic
  • Founders sell when they have no more concrete visions for their company, in which case the buyers probably overpaid. Founders with robust plans don’t sell, meaning offer wasn’t high enough
  • A business with a definite plan will always be underrated in a world where people see the future as random
  • You have agency over your life and a small slice of the world. You’re not random!

Venture Capital
  • Venture capital returns follow power law - small handful of companies vastly outperform others. Firms only invest in companies that have potential to return value of the entire fund.

Look for Secrets
  • Unabomber philosophy, 3 types of human goals:
    1. Goals that can be satisfied with minimal effort
    2. Goals that can be satisfied with serious effort
    3. Goals that cannot be satisfied, no matter how much effort one makes
  • Unabomber argued that all hard problems have already been solved, thus people are depressed.
  • Why does society think there are no secrets left?
    1. Incrementalism: progress made in small, known steps
    2. Risk aversion: people are scared of being wrong, thus don’t even start to look for secrets
    3. Complacency: if you’re already in an elite institution, why look for secrets?
    4. Flatness: if there was something to discover, someone else would have done it since the world is flat/competitive

💡 If you find a secret, only tell whoever you need to, and no more.



Foundations of a Startup
  • Choose board members very carefully, size of 3 is ideal, don’t exceed 5
  • Clearest pattern Thiel’s noticed is a company does better the less it pays its CEO, since cash poor CEO is focused on increasing value of the company. Equity is best way to keep everyone in company broadly aligned
  • Recruiting: should never be outsourced. When pitching, you should highlight company mission, the team, things specific to this startup only
  • Every employee should clearly be responsible for ONE thing.

Sales

Sales is very important.

💡 Superior sales and distribution by itself can create a monopoly, even with no product differentiation. The converse is not true. No matter how strong your product...you must still support it with a strong distribution plan


Two metrics for distribution:

  1. Customer Lifetime Value: total net profit earned on average over customer lifetime
  2. Customer Acquisition Cost: amount you spend on average to acquire new customer

    3. (And CLV > CAC)

Types of Sales Strategy:

  • Complex Sales: Average 1M+ deals which take multiple months of relationships to cultivate. works best when you don’t have a salesman - CEOs talking directly to other CEOs
    • Example: SpaceX, Palentir
  • Personal Sales: deal size of 10-100k. CEO doesn’t do the selling. Challenge here is setting a process up for sales team of modest size to succeed with a wide audience
    • Example: Box sold product to Stanford Sleep Clinic where researchers used it
  • Dead zone between personal sales and traditional advertising - product price point of 1k, no good distribution channel to reach small businesses who are your customer
  • Marketing and Advertising: works well for low priced products with mass appeal but no viral distribution method
    • Example: Proctor and Gamble products
  • Viral Marketing: product is viral if it encourages users to invite friends to become users too. Cheap and fast

Other:

  • Distribution law of sales: nailing one distribution channel is great! If you try multiple channels and don’t land any, you’re done.
  • Must sell to non-customers like media as well.

GreenTech 2012 Bubble Explained

Most cleantech companies failed because they neglected the below:

  1. Engineering: can you create breakthrough tech instead of incremental tech?
  2. Timing: is right now the best time?
  3. Monopoly: Are you starting with big share of small market?
  4. People: do you have the right team?
  5. Distribution: Can you deliver your product?
  6. Durability: Is this defensible in 10, 20 years?
  7. Secret: have you found a unique opportunity that others don’t see?

Other:

  • Most companies did not have x10 technology improvements, meaning no improvement at all for the end user. Customers don’t care that a new technology solves the problem in a superior way, they care about results.
  • Myth of social entrepreneurship: usually fail to do either “do well by doing good”
  • Tesla is an exception to cleantech companies which have failed
