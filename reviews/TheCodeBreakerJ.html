Book Review

The Code Breaker: Jennifer Doudna, Gene Editing, and the Future of the Human Race

By Walter Isaacson



Overview

This book was perfect for someone like me, who has little prior biochemistry knowledge. Isaacson strung together a strong narrative while providing the necessary context to gain a baseline understanding of the science. It is an expansive yet accessible book, ranging from the discovery of DNA‚Äôs double stranded structure, to Jennifer Dounda‚Äôs upbringing, to the ethical questions tied to genetic editing. This is my first Isaacson book, and I want to read more by him.

Aside from the factual reporting, Isaacson flags key ethical questions. Most people would agree to use genetic engineering to alleviate disabilities, but how should we define disability? Where is the line drawn between a disability and a trait, and how does that line relate to how accepting society is of the particular disability? ie, if disability x is more accepted than disability y, is it more ‚Äúok‚Äù to edit for disability y?

Should any genetic edits be evaluated with consideration to value to society? He raises a few examples of notable artists, scientists, and writers who attribute success (ie the spark of creativity, ability to persevere through challenge, etc) to their disability, and begs us to ponder if these contributions to society would have occurred if we allowed people to edit out disabilities at face value. What if you edit a child to have superIQ, and later she brings breakthrough value to the world - is this allowed, since this was an absolute improvement, even though the child gained a relative edge?

Other issues raised include the risk of reducing diversity if parents are inclined to edit to what is conventionally ‚Äúdesirable,‚Äù and the obvious likelihood that genetic engineering is guarded by wealth and thus widens the inequity gap. I wish Isaacson shared more perspectives from key contributors on this topic, but the thought experiments definitely sparked discussion with my friends and I.

Having examined other innovators like Da Vinci, Jobs, and Einstein, Isaacson draws parallels from the CRISPR narrative to these other worlds. Although I value identifying themes across subjects, this came across as a bit forced and unnecessary. I also wonder how biased the book is, as the story reads with a slight tilt against Zhang and co (rightfully so perhaps, but worth calling out that a non-fiction can still very much be subjective).

Overall, I gained exactly what I was seeking when I picked this book up: a digest-able and comprehensive tour of CRISPR and its related technologies. I‚Äôd recommend for anyone else looking for the same.

The DNA Basics üß¨
  • RNA inference: Messenger RNAs build proteins. RNA inference = ‚Äúdicer‚Äù enzyme which targets messenger RNA (via matching sequence) & chops it up, thus intended protein isn‚Äôt built
  • RNA: molecule which reads DNA sequence to build proteins
  • Enzyme: protein, catalyst for critical chemical reactions, found in living organisms ranging from bacteria to humans.
What is CRISPR?
  • CRISPR: Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats. Inspired by bacteria‚Äôs immune system: bacteria attack viruses by storing the virus‚Äô genetic code in their own DNA. tracrRNA latches onto virus, crRNA guides, via genetic code, the Cas enzymes (think scissors) to attack virus.
    • Cas enzyme
      • Cas1: Cas enzyme present in all bacteria that contain CRISPR
      • Cas6: slicing long RNA sequences into shorter chunks
      • Cas12a: makes targeted cuts to DNA. After target is acquired, will chop any nearby single stranded DNA
      • Cas13: similar to Cas12a, will chop up things after cleaving target - but will chop nearby RNA
    • crRNA: CRISPR RNA, genetic RNA coding of virus
    • tracrRNA: creates crRNA, latches onto the virus
    • Originally works in single cell organisms with no nucleus
  • How is CRISPR different than existing gene editing methods?
    • Existing method: double stranded break with a constructed protein as the guide to target specific genetic sequence
    • CRISPR: guide = snippet of RNA

Vaccine
  • Traditional vaccines stimulate the immune system by delivering substance similar to virus/pathogen to patient to make antibodies (either a weakened or killed virus)
  • Invented in 1790s by Edward Jenner (Englishman) who noticed milkmaids were immune to smallpox bc they were previously exposed to cowpox (which didn‚Äôt affect humans)
  • Genetic vaccines: deliver genetic coding directly to human cell so they can produce components of virus, as opposed to injecting virus to patient. Benefit is immune system responses are erratic, better to directly target virus. Methods:
    • J&J vaccine: engineer into a harmless virus the desired component
    • DNA vaccine: give cell DNA/RNA to manufacture vaccine itself. Challenge = DNA delivery as it is housed in nucleus
    • RNA vaccine: easier to deliver than DNA vaccines as RNA is in cytoplasm (this is Pfizer+Moderna vaccines)
    • Ultimate solution: use CRISPR to guide scissors to directly chop up virus (like bacteria does)
      • Cameron Myhrvold created the CARVER system
      • Stanley Qi created PAC-MAN system
